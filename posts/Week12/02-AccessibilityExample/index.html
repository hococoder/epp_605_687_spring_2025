Example

OK, I’ve got the Week 2 view from the Example app loaded on screen, since this gives us a good look at the different types of elements SwiftUI provides.

A few improvements here compared to the video: Accessibility Inspector now properly works with VoiceOver so you can hear the labels spoken out loud for your on screen elements. Also, the video shows a different example from what is below but the concepts are the same - the code below should be considered the most up to date

Adding Labels for Voiceover

In this example, we’re going to focus on how to get your app ready for VoiceOver, which is a specialized screen reader for iOS. To help with this, we can use the Accessibility Inspector that comes with Xcode. To open that tool, go to the Xcode menu, choose Additional Tools, and select Accessibility Inspector.

In the process drop down box, choose the simulator. Let’s get the simulator and inspector side by side. In the inspector, the “Inspection” tab is selected, and to take a look at the elements in our app, let’s select the crosshairs button in the center of the toolbar. With this button selected, we can hover the mouse over the elements in our app and information about them will get presented in the Inspection tab. As I roll the mouse over sections of the app they are highlighted in green, and information such as the accessibility labels, traits, and hierarchy are displayed in the inspector. One thing that you may have noticed is that the Image does not have a very descriptive label for voiceover to read. When describing your app to a person with visual disabilities, including things like the images is important. We can change that in our Week 2 by using a different initializer:

Image("cook_hero", label: Text("Tim Cook"))
  .resizable()
  .aspectRatio(contentMode: .fit)

If we rerun the Example app with this change, and hover over the image, we see the label we specified. You can even prompt VoiceOver to read the labels by pressing on the button that looks like it has a "chat" bubble inside it. Then when you hover over the element, VoiceOver will read the label out loud, so you can make sure it sounds right.

Images that use system names can use the accessibility modifier, by the way:

Image(systemName: "folder.badge.minus")
  .accessibility(label: Text("Delete Folder"))

The accessibility modifier can also be used for other elements such as Text views and Buttons.

Text("Hello this is the accessibility example")
  .accessibility(label: Text("A label stating this view is for the accessibility example"))
Button("Press me!") {
  //do things here
}
.accessibility(label: Text("A button with the label Press Me that can perform certain functions"))

Running Audits

In the Accessibility Inspector, we can also choose the second button in the upper right hand corner - this is the Audit button. This will let us get a report of things that could be improved in our app to make it more accessible. If you push Run audit, you’ll see that we have some issues, some of which are even centered around the simulator itself! You can get a screenshot of the particular element in question, and also get tips for fixing the problem.

Changing Settings

One more thing to look at in the Accessibility Inspector, and that is the Settings button, which can be accessed from the third button in the upper right hand corner of the tool. Here, you can toggle common accessibility options in your app such as inverting the colors, reducing transparency and motion, and increasing contrast. Dynamic text size used to be an option here, but you can see how your elements respond to dynamic type right in the preview canvas.

Conclusion

Getting ready for voiceover is just one of many ways you can get your app accessible. Colors, closed captioning (and other aid for the hearing impaired), bold text, speech, and more can be set for both native UI elements and your custom elements.

Accessibility Inspector gives you a quick way to see how VoiceOver sees your UI elements on screen, along with some reporting tools and easy toggles so you don’t have to go into the settings of the simulator or your device to toggle these modes.
